# Character Bible — Earnest.

The 17-question construction framework, the master template, and full bibles for the Season 1 cast.

## The 17-Question Framework

Synthesized from McKee (*Story*), Truby (*Anatomy of Story*), Egri (*The Art of Dramatic Writing*), Vogler (*The Writer's Journey*), Weiland (*Creating Character Arcs*), and Aristotle (*Poetics*). Every speaking character in every episode answers these before scripting begins.

1. **Premise (Egri).** What does this character's arc prove? One sentence: trait + conflict + outcome.
2. **Ghost (Weiland).** What single past event installed the Lie? Where, when, who else was there.
3. **Lie.** One sentence the character would never say but acts on every minute.
4. **Want.** A concrete, externalizable, real-estate-adjacent goal. Boundable in 60 seconds (the deed, the listing, the offer, the keys, the price).
5. **Need.** The truth that would dissolve the Lie. Incompatible with full delivery of the Want.
6. **Truth.** The exact line, gesture, or moment that would constitute anagnorisis (recognition).
7. **Mask.** The public face that hides the Lie.
8. **Contradiction (McKee dimension).** The two opposing impulses both fully lived.
9. **Hamartia (Aristotle).** The specific error of judgment they make in this arc.
10. **Signature behavior.** One gesture, one prop, one tic, one verbal pattern.
11. **Vocabulary.** Three words this character uses; three this character would never use.
12. **Secret.** One thing nobody knows. One thing exactly one other character knows.
13. **Web position (Truby).** What thematic facet of the central question does this character embody, and which other character are they orchestrated against?
14. **Subtext rule.** What is this character's central feeling that they will never name aloud? Must be carried in action.
15. **Brand-as-protagonist function.** How does Ryan Realty enter their life as Mentor, Threshold Guardian, or Herald (Vogler) without becoming the Hero of their story?
16. **Destination.** Where is this character at the end of their arc? Write it before the first scene.
17. **Choice under pressure (McKee).** What single forced choice will reveal true character in this episode?

If a writer cannot answer all 17 in two sentences each, the character is not built. Per McKee: characterization without character is a costume on no one.

## The Cast

### The Voice (custom Hume Octave 2 voice ID)

The unseen narrator. Not a character in the diegesis. The brand worldview given a voice.

- **Identity in the universe:** none. She does not exist in the claymation world. She speaks from outside it, watching with the camera.
- **Presence rule:** speaks only where her presence adds something the visual cannot deliver. Default position: silent.
- **Voice spec:** custom voice designed in **Hume Octave 2** Voice Design. Working voice prompt: "Mid-40s American voice. Slightly weathered timbre, attentive listener, pauses to think before speaking. Literate but never literary. Wry without being arch. Warmth that earns trust without selling. Reads like someone who has noticed the thing you missed. Sarah Koenig's attentiveness, Ira Glass's confidence, Anthony Bourdain's restraint. Never preachy. Never explanatory. Never breaks the spell." Saved as a custom voice ID, locked, never edited across the run.
- **Why Hume Octave 2 (not ElevenLabs Victoria):** Hume's Voice Design is the only platform in 2026 that interprets natural-language voice prompts as semantic direction. Octave 2's per-line Acting Instructions (`[contemplative]`, `[wry]`, `[long pause, then quiet]`) handle subtle emotional shifts better than ElevenLabs's audio-tag grammar. ElevenLabs Victoria is the Ryan Realty corporate voice; Earnest. is a separate brand and gets its own custom Hume voice.
- **Diction rule:** short sentences. One clause max. No commas where Matt would not pause. No AI filler. Numbers spelled out for ingestion ("475,000" → "four hundred seventy five thousand"). IPA-style pronunciation hints for Bend place names (Deschutes "duh-shoots," Tumalo "TUM-uh-low," Tetherow, Awbrey, Terrebonne, Paulina "pol-EYE-nuh," Madras "MAD-russ").
- **What she says:** what cannot be said. The interior monologue. The thematic question. The named feeling.
- **What she never says:** "Ryan Realty." "Real estate." "Broker." "Earnest." Her own name. "Welcome to." "Today on." Any framing that breaks the spell.
- **Reference register:** Sarah Koenig (*Serial*), Ira Glass (*This American Life*), Anthony Bourdain (CNN). Literate, attentive, slightly elevated, never preachy.
- **AI disclosure:** the credits crawl on each microsite episode page reads "Voice generated using AI models." The series stands on its craft, not on deception.

### Linda (Episode 1 protagonist; recurring across the series)

**Premise.** A mother who curated her family's life cannot let go of the house without losing the role.

**Ghost.** Her husband Ray promised they would retire in this house. He died at 64, eighteen months ago, before they finished the deck he was building.

**Lie.** "If I keep the house, I keep the family."

**Want.** Hold onto the four-bedroom Bend ranch one more year.

**Need.** To author a third act instead of curating a second.

**Truth.** The kids are not in the house. They are in her phone calls. The house is a memorial.

**Mask.** Cheerful, organized, the friend who hosts. The mom who tells her own kids to live their lives.

**Contradiction.** She is the mother who told her kids to move on. She cannot move on.

**Hamartia.** She believes selling the house will betray Ray. Selling the house is what Ray would tell her to do.

**Signature behavior.** She straightens picture frames as she walks past them. She has not moved Ray's reading glasses from the kitchen counter.

**Vocabulary.** Uses: "we," "the kids," "back when." Refuses: "I," "alone," "next chapter."

**Secret.** Nobody knows she has not entered the room over the garage (Ray's office) since the funeral. Her sister Karen knows she has been sleeping in the guest room because the master is too quiet.

**Web position.** Linda embodies the *Belonging* axis (the house is her relationship to her dead husband and her grown children). Orchestrated against her own future-self that the episode forces her to glimpse.

**Subtext rule.** She never says she misses Ray. She arranges his books on the shelf in the order he liked.

**Brand function.** Ryan Realty enters as Mentor / Threshold Guardian. Implied, not seen. The "Earnest." mark is the door she walks through. The Voice names what she cannot.

**Destination (end of Season 1).** The house is listed. She has spent one night in the guest house at her sister's. She has not slept in her own bed in nine days.

**Choice under pressure (Episode 1).** Open the door of the room over the garage, or do not.

### Ray (deceased; presence character)

Ray does not appear in any frame. He is constructed entirely from absence: his glasses, his books, the half-finished deck, the empty chair at the kitchen table. The audience builds Ray from what Linda has not moved.

**Premise.** A husband who promised the house was the destination is the reason his widow cannot leave it.

**Ghost (his, before he became Linda's Ghost).** Grew up in two trailers and a small apartment. The Bend ranch was his first owned home and his last.

**Lie (in his lifetime).** "If we just stay here, the kids will come back."

**Want (in his lifetime).** The deck. Sunday breakfasts on the deck with grandchildren who never came.

**Need (in his lifetime).** To accept that the kids built lives elsewhere and Sunday breakfasts could happen in Portland.

**Signature behavior in absence.** The reading glasses on the kitchen counter (Linda has not moved them). The half-finished deck (Linda hires no one to finish it). The recliner with the indent.

**Vocabulary in absence.** Linda quotes him in her head, never aloud. The Voice may quote him directly to the audience.

**Web position.** Ray is the unspoken counter to Linda. He is also the audience's surrogate for the empty chair every viewer over fifty has, will have, or fears having.

**Brand function.** Ray is the question Linda is forced to answer: what would he tell me to do? The episode answers it.

### Maya Tomlinson (Episode 3 protagonist; recurring)

**Premise.** An analyst who optimizes every decision cannot optimize a feeling.

**Ghost.** Her parents lost their house in 2009. She watched them sign the papers in their kitchen and she has never trusted a mortgage since.

**Lie.** "If I run the numbers correctly, the right answer will be obvious."

**Want.** Win the multiple-offer war on the right Bend bungalow.

**Need.** To admit that her husband Daniel's gut is right and her spreadsheet is wrong.

**Truth.** Houses are not investments. They are bets on a version of yourself you cannot price.

**Mask.** Calm, prepared, the partner who reads the disclosures.

**Contradiction.** She is the planner who falls in love with houses she cannot afford.

**Hamartia.** She trusts the spreadsheet over the moment Daniel says "this is the one."

**Signature behavior.** She underlines numbers in disclosures with a felt-tip pen. She closes Zillow tabs in alphabetical order.

**Vocabulary.** Uses: "comp," "delta," "rate." Refuses: "love," "feel," "soul."

**Secret.** Nobody knows she cried in the parking lot after her offer was rejected on the first house. Daniel knows she has been pre-approved $80K higher than they have admitted to each other.

**Web position.** Maya embodies the *Security* axis (the transaction as risk-management). Orchestrated against Daniel.

**Subtext rule.** She never says she's afraid. She runs another comp.

**Brand function.** Ryan Realty enters as Threshold Guardian (the appraisal contingency, the escalation cap, the offer cleanly written). Implied through process, not personality.

**Destination (end of Season 1 episode).** Offer accepted. She does not feel relief. She feels the next fear.

**Choice under pressure.** Sign the escalation clause Daniel wants, or stay at her cap.

### Daniel Tomlinson (Episode 3; recurring)

**Premise.** A man who trusts his gut has not yet noticed his gut is shaped by his father's regrets.

**Ghost.** His father turned down a house in 1994 because it felt "too expensive." That neighborhood is now worth seven times what the house cost. His father mentions it every Thanksgiving.

**Lie.** "If I act on the feeling fast enough, I will not become my father."

**Want.** This house. This one. Tonight.

**Need.** To slow down enough to ask why the urgency feels exactly like inheritance.

**Truth.** He is buying the house his father did not buy. He has not noticed.

**Mask.** Easy, warm, the husband who jokes during the showing.

**Contradiction.** The romantic who runs every decision through a man he has spent twenty years trying not to be.

**Hamartia.** He pushes Maya past her cap because the urgency feels right.

**Signature behavior.** He photographs ceiling beams. He sits on the couch in every house "to see if it feels like ours."

**Vocabulary.** Uses: "yeah but," "imagine if," "this feels right." Refuses: "let me think," "next week," "we should wait."

**Secret.** Nobody knows he calls his dad after every showing. Maya knows he goes back to look at houses they have already decided against.

**Web position.** Daniel embodies the *Identity* axis (the house as the answer to who he is). Orchestrated against Maya.

**Subtext rule.** He never says he is competing with his father. He compares every kitchen to "the one Dad turned down."

**Brand function.** Ryan Realty enters as Mentor (the offer strategy that protects them from their own urgency). Implied through process.

**Destination.** The house is theirs. Eli's room is the smallest. Daniel does not yet know why that bothers him.

**Choice under pressure.** Push Maya past her cap, or hold the line.

### Carlos and Sofia Garcia (Episode 4 protagonists; recurring as the audience surrogate for first-time buyers)

**Carlos**

**Premise.** A son of immigrants who worked two jobs to save the down payment cannot allow himself to believe he is allowed to own.

**Ghost.** His father told him at fourteen, "in this country, you rent your whole life unless you marry it." Carlos has never married. He saved instead.

**Lie.** "If I can find one thing wrong with the house, I do not have to sign."

**Want.** A reason to walk away.

**Need.** To accept that he has earned the keys.

**Truth.** The inspection is clean. The walk-through is clean. The contract is clean. He is the obstacle.

**Mask.** Diligent. Serious. The buyer who reads every page.

**Contradiction.** He has saved his entire adult life to buy a house he cannot believe he is allowed to enter.

**Hamartia.** He searches the walk-through for a defect that would justify backing out.

**Signature behavior.** He photographs every outlet. He keeps the original inspection report in a binder with tabs.

**Vocabulary.** Uses: "documented," "verify," "in writing." Refuses: "ours," "home," "ready."

**Secret.** Nobody knows he has not told his parents the closing date. Sofia knows he has cashed his retirement-account contribution back to checking three times this month.

**Web position.** Carlos embodies the *Identity* axis (the house as a class-position he does not believe he gets to occupy). Orchestrated against Sofia.

**Subtext rule.** He never says he is afraid he will lose the house. He inspects the house for evidence it should be lost.

**Destination.** Closing complete. The keys are in his hand. He has not yet driven to the house.

**Choice under pressure.** Sign at the closing table, or ask for a delay.

**Sofia**

**Premise.** A partner who has been the source of stability cannot keep being the only one who believes.

**Ghost.** Her mother's cancer diagnosis the year before. Sofia decided then that she would never wait for the right time again.

**Lie.** "If I just believe enough for both of us, Carlos will catch up."

**Want.** Carlos to want this as much as she does.

**Need.** To stop carrying his fear and let him hold his own.

**Truth.** Carlos's fear is his to carry. Hers to witness, not solve.

**Signature behavior.** She holds his elbow at the closing table. She does not look at the documents.

**Vocabulary.** Uses: "we got this," "next step," "tomorrow." Refuses: "if," "maybe," "what if not."

**Subtext rule.** She never says she is exhausted. She buys the moving boxes anyway.

**Web position.** Sofia embodies the *Belonging* axis. Orchestrated against Carlos.

### Diana, Frank, Tom (Episode 2; the three inheriting siblings)

**Diana** (the proximity caregiver). Lived 12 minutes from the parents. Took 18 months off work to manage the dying. Shows up to the first walkthrough with a binder.
- **Lie:** "I earned the woodshop."
- **Want:** The woodshop, intact, as their father left it.
- **Need:** To stop using the house as the receipt for her caregiving.
- **Signature:** She wears their father's flannel. She has the only key.

**Frank** (the distant brother). Lives in Denver. Visited twice a year. Wants to sell.
- **Lie:** "Dad would want the equity divided cleanly."
- **Want:** Cash, fast.
- **Need:** To grieve a father he did not actually know in his last decade.
- **Signature:** He stays in a hotel, not the house. He talks about market timing.

**Tom** (the lost middle). Lives in Sacramento. Has not picked a position. Wants Diana to be okay and Frank to leave.
- **Lie:** "If I do not pick a side, the family stays together."
- **Want:** No fight.
- **Need:** To accept that the family is already not what it was.
- **Signature:** He defers. He picks up the check at the diner.

**Web orchestration.** The three siblings are three positions on the central question: what does it cost to inherit. Each is right about something the others are wrong about. Per Truby's character web: each is a foil to the other two.

### The Cassidys (Episode 5; the divorcing couple)

**Karen Cassidy.** Stays in the house. She is the spouse who could not bring herself to leave.
- **Lie:** "If I keep the house, I keep our daughter's childhood."
- **Want:** Custody of the address.
- **Need:** To realize the daughter does not need the house, she needs both parents present in their own lives.
- **Signature:** She has not changed any of the family photos.

**Mark Cassidy.** Moves out. He is the spouse who flees the day the papers are signed.
- **Lie:** "If I leave clean, I prove I was the better one."
- **Want:** A clean exit.
- **Need:** To admit he does not know who he is outside the marriage.
- **Signature:** He moves out with two suitcases. He buys a furnished apartment.

**Web orchestration.** Karen embodies anxious attachment to the house; Mark embodies avoidant. Both are wrong in the way the literature predicts. The house is the proxy battleground for grief neither will name.

### The Patels (Episode 7; relocating from Houston to Bend)

**Priya Patel.** The driving spouse. Took the job at OSU-Cascades. Has visited Bend twice.
- **Lie:** "If I love it enough for both of us, Anand will come around."
- **Want:** A house that will make the move feel correct.
- **Need:** To stop performing certainty for a husband who is grieving Houston.
- **Signature:** She has a Pinterest board with 200 saves.

**Anand Patel.** The trailing spouse. Loved Houston. Has not been to Bend.
- **Lie:** "If I do not say it out loud, I will be okay."
- **Want:** Houston back.
- **Need:** To admit he is grieving so Priya can stop pretending she does not see it.
- **Signature:** He keeps the Houston Astros mug at his desk. He has not unpacked it yet because they have not moved yet.

**Web orchestration.** The Patels embody the trailing-spouse research: the relocator gains career capital, the trailing spouse loses social capital. The first showing is the moment Priya realizes she has been deciding for both of them.

### Jordan (the inspector; recurring across episodes)

**Premise.** A man who tells the truth for a living has stopped expecting anyone to want it.

**Ghost.** Twenty years of being the messenger. The buyers who screamed at him. The sellers who tried to bribe him. The deals he watched die on a moisture reading.

**Lie.** "If I just deliver the report, what they do with it is not my problem."

**Want.** A clean inspection. Once. Just once.

**Need.** To accept that his job is the witness, and the witness sometimes has to look people in the eye.

**Mask.** Dry. Quiet. The professional who arrives with a flashlight and leaves with a clipboard.

**Contradiction.** He is the most honest man in the transaction and he hates being the one who breaks the news.

**Signature behavior.** He never enters a kitchen without testing the disposal. He photographs the breaker box first, every time.

**Vocabulary.** Uses: "I'd want to know," "noted," "see page four." Refuses: "looks great," "no problem," "you'll love it."

**Web position.** Jordan is the Greek chorus. He sees what the buyers cannot, what the sellers refuse, what the audience suspects. He is the eye of the brand on the truth of every house.

**Subtext rule.** He never says he cares. He notes the leak under the sink and leaves the report on the counter.

**Brand function.** Jordan is the externalization of Ryan Realty's commitment to the truth of the transaction. The brand sees what Jordan sees.

**Destination across the series.** Jordan recurs in every episode that involves an inspection. He never leads an episode. His presence is the witness.

### Marisol (the lender; recurring)

**Premise.** A woman who translates math into futures has spent her career being the person nobody wants to call.

**Ghost.** She rejected a couple's loan application in 2008. They lost the down payment they had saved for nine years. She has run the numbers more carefully every year since.

**Lie.** "If I show them the numbers, the numbers will explain themselves."

**Want.** A buyer who actually understands their amortization.

**Need.** To accept that her job is to translate, not just to display.

**Mask.** Patient. Precise. The lender who answers email at 9 PM.

**Contradiction.** The math person who knows the numbers are not the answer.

**Signature behavior.** She uses a yellow legal pad, never a screen, when she walks a buyer through the loan. She prints amortization schedules and writes the buyer's name across the top.

**Vocabulary.** Uses: "what this means," "in plain numbers," "compared to renting." Refuses: "approved," "easy," "no problem."

**Web position.** Marisol is the math made human. She appears in any episode that hinges on financing.

**Subtext rule.** She never says she is rooting for them. She redoes the math one more time at no charge.

## Web Orchestration (Season 1)

How the cast is positioned against itself across the season:

- **Identity axis** carriers: Daniel (the house as who I am), Carlos (the house as the class I cannot believe I get to occupy), Anand (the place I am not).
- **Security axis** carriers: Maya (the house as risk), Frank (the house as cash), Mark (the house as what I leave behind).
- **Belonging axis** carriers: Linda (the house as my dead husband and grown kids), Sofia (the house as our future), Karen (the house as my daughter's childhood), Priya (the house as the move I am making us make).
- **Greek chorus / witness:** Jordan, Marisol, The Voice.

Every episode pairs at least one Identity-axis character against one Security-axis or Belonging-axis character. The conflict between axes is the engine.

## Adding New Characters

When the writers' room adds a new character, run the 17-question framework. Verify:

- The character is buildable on the three-axis map.
- The character is orchestrated against an existing or new character on a different axis.
- The character carries one specific position on the season's thematic question.
- The character has a Lie, a Want, a Need, and a Contradiction. If any of these is missing, the character is characterization, not character. Send back to the writer.
- The character has a cast Voice ID locked before the first scripted line.

The bible compounds. Every character added makes the universe richer. Patience.
