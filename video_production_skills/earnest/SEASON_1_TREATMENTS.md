# Season 1 Treatments — Earnest.

Season 1 thematic question: **"Why is this so hard?"**

Seven self-contained episodes. Each a different position on the question. Each anchored to a real human-emotion dynamic from the academic literature on housing decisions. Episode 1 fully drafted to script. Episodes 2-7 treated at outline depth, scriptable when production reaches them.

## Episode 1: "The Empty Room"

**Target:** 60 seconds, 1080×1920, 30 fps.
**Featured characters:** Linda (Lie/Want/Need/Truth/Ghost in `CHARACTER_BIBLE.md`), Ray (presence), The Voice.
**Educational beat (save-bait):** the emotional reality of downsizing as grief, plus the practical concept of a "leave-behind list."

### Logline

### Educational Beat (save-bait)

The visual subtext ("She has not been in this room since the funeral") delivers the emotional reality of downsizing-as-grief without lecturing. The save-bait is the comment-keyword CTA in the platform caption: "Comment DOWNSIZE for the empty-nester playbook." The lead magnet covers the leave-behind list, the 90-day decluttering arc, the practical and emotional sequencing of a downsize.

### Cliffhanger / Button

The held hand on the unturned doorknob. The audience does not see what is behind the door. They feel the weight of what is. Cut on the question, not the answer.

### Why This Is the Right Pilot

- Universal. Every viewer either is, has, or will be Linda or her kids. Hooks the widest possible cold audience.
- Demonstrates the brand discipline: silence over narration, gesture over dialogue, gravity over gimmick. If we can sell this episode, we can sell any episode.
- Sets the bar for craft. A 60-second episode with twelve beats, two lines of narration, and one held shot at the end is a statement about what Earnest. is.
- Aligns with the Season 1 thematic question by being the most direct answer: it is hard because love does not stop because the deed transferred.
- Conversion-friendly: the empty-nester audience is high-intent, high-equity, often ready to list within 90 days of starting to think about it.

## Episode 2: "The Garage"

**Target:** 55 seconds.
**Featured characters:** Diana, Frank, Tom (full bibles in `CHARACTER_BIBLE.md`), The Voice.
**Educational beat:** the inheritance principle that disposition decisions (sell/keep/rent) should be made BEFORE object decisions (woodshop/recipe-box/photos). Per Pillemer's Cornell Legacy Project research: 70% of estate disputes are about non-monetary objects.
**CTA:** `INHERIT` → inheritance disposition guide.

### Logline

Three siblings stand in their dead father's garage. Two want to sell the house. One wants the woodshop. The fight is not about the woodshop.

### The Beat Structure

- Cold open: hard cut from "Earnest." mark to the three siblings standing in a Lego garage doorway, dim overhead light, dust in the air.
- Setup: each sibling identified through a single piece of business. Diana wears the father's flannel. Frank checks his Lego phone. Tom hangs back, hands in pockets.
- Pressure: Frank says, "we should list it." Diana says, "I want the woodshop." Tom says nothing.
- The Voice (one line): "The fight is not about the woodshop."
- Visual reveal: tight shot of Diana's hand on a wooden bench. The grain of the wood. Her father's initials carved into the corner.
- Cliffhanger: Diana looks up at her brothers. Cut before she speaks.

### Why This Episode

Inheritance is the single most under-served real-estate topic in social content. The drama is built into the family dynamic. The educational payoff (sequence disposition before objects) is universally applicable.

## Episode 3: "The Counter"

**Target:** 50 seconds.
**Featured characters:** Maya and Daniel Tomlinson (bibles in `CHARACTER_BIBLE.md`), The Voice.
**Educational beat:** the multiple-offer dynamic. Escalation clauses, appraisal gaps, and the discipline of a cap.
**CTA:** `OFFER` → multiple-offer playbook.

### Logline

In a coffee shop on Bond Street, a couple talks about money. They mean something else.

### The Beat Structure

- Cold open: tight on a Lego coffee cup. Maya's hand pushes a Lego folder across the table to Daniel.
- Setup: Daniel flips through the disclosures. Maya watches.
- Pressure: Daniel says, "let's go to 525." Maya says, "we said 510." Daniel says, "I know what we said."
- The Voice: "She is not arguing about fifteen thousand dollars."
- Visual reveal: Maya's notebook, open on the seat beside her. A column of numbers. The top number is the family budget. The bottom number is what her parents lost in 2009.
- Cliffhanger: Maya picks up the pen. Cut before she signs or refuses.

### Why This Episode

Bidding wars are the most-searched real estate topic in 2026. The drama is the universal couples-and-money tension. The educational payoff (the cap discipline) is what every buyer needs.

## Episode 4: "The Walk-Through"

**Target:** 55 seconds.
**Featured characters:** Carlos and Sofia Garcia (bibles in `CHARACTER_BIBLE.md`), Jordan (cameo), The Voice.
**Educational beat:** what to look for in a final walk-through. Every functioning fixture, every documented repair, the inspector's punchlist closed.
**CTA:** `INSPECT` → final walk-through checklist.

### Logline

A first-time-buying couple walks the empty house the night before closing. He is looking for a reason to walk away. She is looking at him.

### The Beat Structure

- Cold open: Carlos and Sofia at the front door. Carlos with the binder. Sofia empty-handed.
- Setup: Carlos checks every outlet. Sofia stands in the middle of what will be the kitchen.
- Pressure: Carlos says, "the disposal is loose." Sofia says, "Jordan signed it off." Carlos says, "I want to verify."
- The Voice: "He is not looking for a defect. He is looking for permission to keep being safe."
- Visual reveal: Sofia holds out the keys, which Marisol couriered ahead. Carlos has not seen them yet.
- Cliffhanger: Carlos's hand reaches for the keys. Cut before he takes them.

## Episode 5: "The Closing Table"

**Target:** 55 seconds.
**Featured characters:** Karen and Mark Cassidy (bibles in `CHARACTER_BIBLE.md`), The Voice.
**Educational beat:** the divorce-divestment process. Dividing equity. Refinance options. Who keeps what.
**CTA:** `SETTLE` → divorce-divestment guide.

### Logline

In a small office on Wall Street downtown, two people sign a stack of documents. They do not sign at the same time.

### The Beat Structure

- Cold open: oval Lego conference table, two pens, two stacks of documents, two Lego water bottles.
- Setup: Karen on one side. Mark on the other. Mark signs first. Karen waits.
- Pressure: Mark slides his stack across. Karen does not look at him.
- The Voice (one line in two parts):
  - "He is signing to end it."
  - (later) "She is signing because she has not yet."
- Visual reveal: Karen's pen hovers over the line. Her wedding ring catches the fluorescent light.
- Cliffhanger: Karen's pen touches the page. Cut before the signature lands.

### Why This Episode

Divorce is the single most under-discussed real-estate scenario. The drama is built in. The educational payoff (the sequence and finance of dividing the marital home) is high-intent.

## Episode 6: "The Open House"

**Target:** 60 seconds.
**Featured characters:** Six anonymous Lego visitors, the Voice (six interior monologues).
**Educational beat:** the seller's perspective on six visitors and what each one signals. The investor sniffing for distress. The first-time buyer who will write but cannot afford. The neighbor who is not buying. The serious couple who will write tonight. The agent on a tour. The family whose offer died yesterday.
**CTA:** `LIST` → seller's open-house playbook.

### Logline

Six strangers walk into a house on a Sunday afternoon in Bend. Each one is the protagonist of a different story.

### The Beat Structure

The Wire-style ensemble episode. Six 8-second beats. Each visitor gets one Voice line that names what they are not saying.

- Cold open: front door of the open house. Balloon arch. Sign on the lawn (no readable text).
- Beat 1 (8s): the investor. Voice: "He is calculating yield."
- Beat 2 (8s): the first-time buyer. Voice: "She is calculating courage."
- Beat 3 (8s): the neighbor. Voice: "He is here to know what his house is worth."
- Beat 4 (8s): the serious couple. Voice: "They are deciding tonight."
- Beat 5 (8s): the agent on tour. Voice: "She is here for the next showing."
- Beat 6 (8s): the family whose offer died yesterday. Voice: "They are saying goodbye."
- Cliffhanger: the front door closes behind the last visitor. The empty house. Cut on the silence.

### Why This Episode

A structural showpiece. Demonstrates the brand's range and the universe's depth. The "six monologues, one house" structure is the signature episode of the season; the one critics will reference.

## Episode 7: "The First Showing"

**Target:** 60 seconds.
**Featured characters:** Priya and Anand Patel (bibles in `CHARACTER_BIBLE.md`), The Voice.
**Educational beat:** what a first-time-in-Bend buyer needs to know in the first 24 hours. Neighborhoods (Tetherow, Awbrey, Tumalo, NW Crossing, Old Bend). Snow. Smoke season. School districts. Water rights. Fire risk.
**CTA:** `RELOCATE` → Bend relocation guide.

### Logline

A couple from Houston pulls into a Bend cul-de-sac for the first time. The Cascades are visible. Snow has not fallen yet. They have not yet realized this will be their life.

### The Beat Structure

- Cold open: a Lego Subaru pulling into a Lego cul-de-sac. Snow-capped mountains in the background.
- Setup: Priya stepping out, looking at the mountains, hand to her chest. Anand stepping out, looking at his Lego phone.
- Pressure: Priya says, "look at this." Anand looks at his phone. Priya looks at Anand.
- The Voice (one line in two parts):
  - "She is making the move for both of them."
  - (later) "He is grieving Houston."
- Visual reveal: Anand sets down the Lego coffee mug with the Astros logo on the trunk of the Subaru. He has carried it the whole way from Texas.
- Cliffhanger: Priya turns to look at the house. Anand still looks at the mug. Cut on the un-met gaze.

### Why This Episode

The relocator scenario is the brokerage's sweet spot. Out-of-state buyers are higher-margin, longer-term clients. The educational payoff (the Bend reality check) is high-value. The drama (asymmetric move) is built in.

## Production Order

The order in which episodes are produced is not the order in which they release. Suggested production order:

1. **Episode 1 ("The Empty Room")** — pilot, sets the bar, must be produced first to validate the entire pipeline.
2. **Episode 6 ("The Open House")** — structural showpiece. Tests the ensemble workflow and the six-character cycle.
3. **Episode 3 ("The Counter")** — high-volume topic, broad appeal. Validates the couples dynamic.
4. **Episode 2 ("The Garage")** — the inheritance episode. Tests the three-character orchestration.
5. **Episode 5 ("The Closing Table")** — the divorce episode. Tests the high-stakes silence.
6. **Episode 4 ("The Walk-Through")** — the first-time-buyer episode. Tests the Jordan cameo workflow.
7. **Episode 7 ("The First Showing")** — the relocator episode. Tests the Patels' arc and the Bend-specific imagery library.

Production at one episode per week = Season 1 ships in 7 weeks. Release schedule is consecutive days per `DISTRIBUTION.md`.

After all seven episodes ship and the metrics window closes (typically 14 days after the last episode), do a Season 1 retrospective. Document:

- What worked. What did not.
- Which character bibles need expansion before they recur in Season 2.
- Which production-pipeline steps were bottlenecks.
- Which CTAs converted, which did not, and why.

The retrospective is the input to Season 2 planning. Season 2 builds on the universe; it does not start from scratch.
