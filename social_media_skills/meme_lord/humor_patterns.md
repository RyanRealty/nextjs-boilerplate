# Humor Patterns — 35+ Example Concepts by Category

**Purpose:** A reference library of proven meme concepts organized by humor pattern. Every example here either comes directly from a viral RE meme account or is modeled on their exact structure. When generating new concepts, pick a pattern, find a current friction, and follow the structural rules that make that pattern work.

**Read `humor_calibration.md` first.** This file is the examples library. That file is the theory.

---

## Pattern 1: PAINFUL SPECIFICITY (the #1 pattern — use most often)

The joke is a hyper-specific moment that every agent has lived but nobody talks about publicly. The specificity creates the recognition. The recognition creates the share.

**Structure:** Describe a moment with at least 3 concrete details (time, dollar amount, number of days, specific app, specific object, specific phrase a client said).

### Examples:

**1.** "11:47pm. In bed. Eyes closed. Phone buzzes. 'Hey quick question about that house we drove past 6 weeks ago.'"
- Why it works: The time (11:47pm), the physical state (in bed, eyes closed), the absurdity of the request (6 weeks ago, drove past), the casual "hey quick question" framing

**2.** "'We're in no rush' — the buyer who called me 14 times on a Sunday because a house they liked got 3 offers in 2 days"
- Why it works: Exact count (14 times), specific day (Sunday), specific trigger (3 offers, 2 days). The quote vs. reality contrast.

**3.** "Listed at $725K. Offer comes in at $580K. Cover letter says 'We feel this is fair given the current market.' Brother, what market."
- Why it works: Real numbers ($725K/$580K = $145K gap), the verbatim cover letter language, the deadpan "Brother, what market" punchline.

**4.** "Open house. 1-3pm. One person shows up at 2:47. It's the neighbor. She took 6 cookies and told me the previous owners were 'interesting people.'"
- Why it works: Timeline (2:47pm), the count (6 cookies), the passive-aggressive quote.

**5.** "'I don't want to bother you' — the text I got at 11:58pm on a Tuesday asking if a house with an accepted offer could still be shown"
- Why it works: The polite framing vs. the absurd content. The specific time and day. The detail about accepted offer.

**6.** "Got the contract signed. Now I just need the buyer to not change jobs, the appraiser to not lowball it, the lender to not find a 2019 medical collection for $340, the seller to not relist the dishwasher on Facebook Marketplace, and the title company to not discover a 1987 easement nobody knew about."
- Why it works: Escalation through increasingly absurd but real scenarios. Every agent has a story for at least 3 of these.

**7.** "Buyer: 'We've been looking for 8 months and can't find the right house.' Me: *pulls up the 3 houses I showed them in month 1 that were perfect and they said they 'needed to think about'*"
- Why it works: The specific timeline, the specific count, the internal monologue format.

## Pattern 2: ESCALATION (start normal, end absurd)

The setup is reasonable. Each beat adds a layer of chaos. The final beat is absurd but plausible.

**Structure:** 3-5 beats, each more unhinged than the last. The last beat should make the reader think "OK that's too far... but also yes."

### Examples:

**8.** "Stages of a real estate deal: Excitement → Anxiety → The inspection report → Existential dread → 'Actually this is fine' → Closing day tears (unclear if happy or exhausted)"
- Why it works: Emotional escalation that every agent recognizes. "Unclear if happy or exhausted" is the kill shot.

**9.** "Year 1: 'I'm going to be the best agent in town.' Year 2: 'OK this is harder than I thought.' Year 3: 'My car is my office and I keep dress shoes in the trunk.' Year 5: 'I genuinely cannot remember the last time I ate lunch sitting down.'"
- Why it works: Career escalation/degradation arc. Each year is worse but more specific.

**10.** Expanding Brain format: "Looking at Zillow" → "Getting pre-approved" → "Going to 37 showings" → "Writing an offer on the first house you saw"
- Why it works: The escalation tracks a real buyer journey that circles back to the beginning, which is genuinely how it goes.

**11.** Gru's Plan format: "Price the house at market value" → "Get multiple offers in the first weekend" → "Seller decides to take the house off market because 'maybe we should wait'" → *Gru horrified face* → "Seller decides to take the house off market because 'maybe we should wait'"
- Why it works: The plan falling apart at step 3 is painfully common. The repeated panel hits the "no, this is real" button.

**12.** "The evolution of an agent's phone: Contact list of 50 people → 500 people → 5,000 people → still nobody answering when you call"
- Why it works: Numbers escalate but the result stays the same. Dark, relatable.

## Pattern 3: SELF-DEPRECATION (agent roasting themselves)

The agent is the butt of the joke. Never the hero. The humor comes from honest admission of suffering, confusion, or inadequacy.

**Structure:** First-person or "me doing X" format. The agent must look slightly pathetic in a relatable way.

### Examples:

**13.** "Me, a licensed professional with 200 hours of CE credits, nervously googling 'what is a plat map' in my car before a listing appointment"
- Why it works: The contrast between credentials and reality. Every agent has secret-googled something basic.

**14.** "My morning routine: Wake up. Check email. 47 unread. Panic. Check MLS. Nothing new. Check email again. Still 47. Stare at ceiling. Get dressed. Check email in the car. Now 52."
- Why it works: The compulsive checking loop is universal. The specific number climbing (47→52) adds realism.

**15.** "Told myself I'd take Sunday off. It's now 3pm and I've responded to 4 emails, scheduled 2 showings, argued with a lender, and I'm eating cold pizza in the car wearing what I slept in."
- Why it works: The "day off" that isn't. Specific detail cascade. "What I slept in" is the cherry.

**16.** "'How's business?' Great. I showed 6 houses last Saturday, drove 140 miles, none of them wrote an offer, and I spent $38 on gas to make $0."
- Why it works: The polite question vs. the brutal math. Specific numbers make it sting.

**17.** "When someone asks 'What do you do all day?' and the honest answer is 'I send 30 emails, make 15 calls, get ghosted by 12 people, drive 80 miles, and eat one meal standing up.'"
- Why it works: The enumeration reveals the unglamorous reality.

## Pattern 4: VERBATIM CLIENT QUOTES (said with a straight face)

The humor is entirely in what the client said. No editorializing needed. The quote speaks for itself.

**Structure:** "Client said [exact quote]" or iMessage screenshot format. Agent reaction is minimal — let the quote do the work.

### Examples:

**18.** "Client: 'I know you said the market is competitive but can we offer $100K under asking and see what happens?' What happens is the listing agent blocks my number."
- Why it works: Verbatim ask + the deadpan consequence.

**19.** "'We just bought a truck and need to update our pre-approval.' Sir, you were buying a HOUSE."
- Why it works: The casual way clients torpedo their own deals. The capitalized HOUSE for emphasis.

**20.** "'My friend is a realtor too but I wanted to see what you thought.' Cool. So you're getting a second opinion on your second opinion."
- Why it works: Every agent has gotten this call. The sarcastic reframe.

**21.** "'We don't need to get pre-approved yet, we just want to look.' Famous last words. The house they fell in love with sold while their lender was still asking for tax returns."
- Why it works: The specific consequence (sold while lender was processing) is reality.

**22.** "'Can we see this house ASAP? We want to write an offer TODAY.' *shows them the house* 'Actually, we're going to think about it for a few weeks.'"
- Why it works: The urgency-to-ghosting pipeline every agent knows.

**23.** "'We saw this house on TikTok. Can we buy it?' It's in Tennessee."
- Why it works: The absurd disconnect. Short and devastating.

## Pattern 5: DARK MARKET COMMENTARY (gallows humor about conditions)

The market is the antagonist. The agent and client are both victims. The humor is in the shared suffering.

**Structure:** Frame the market condition as a story, not a stat. Use real numbers but wrap them in human experience.

### Examples:

**24.** "2022: 'I'll wait for rates to drop.' 2023: 'Any day now.' 2024: 'The Fed said maybe.' 2025: 'OK maybe I should've bought.' 2026: house costs $80K more, rate is the same, and the buyer is now competing against 4 cash offers from people who sold in Austin."
- Why it works: Year-by-year specificity tells a real story. The Austin cash offers are a painfully specific 2025-2026 phenomenon.

**25.** "Rates dropped 0.25%. Every buyer who's been waiting since 2022 just came out of hibernation at the same time. It's like Black Friday but the thing on sale is a $650K split-level with one bathroom."
- Why it works: The specific rate drop, the Black Friday comparison, the unflattering property description.

**26.** "Months of supply at 2.1. Translation: if you want this house, bring cash, a love letter, your firstborn, and a willingness to waive everything your mother told you to never waive."
- Why it works: Starts with a real stat, translates it into escalating absurdity.

**27.** "'How's the market?' Depends. Are you buying, selling, or just asking so you can tell me what your cousin's friend's Zillow says?"
- Why it works: The three-option structure where the third option is the one that actually happens.

## Pattern 6: FORMAT SUBVERSION (using a template against its grain)

Taking a well-known template and using it in an unexpected way that creates a double-take.

**Structure:** The reader expects the template to go one way. It goes another.

### Examples:

**28.** Two Buttons format: Button 1: "Tell the seller their house is overpriced by $150K" / Button 2: "Take the listing at their price and let the market teach them for 97 days"
- Why it works: Both options are bad. The sweating man format is perfect for genuine impossible choices.

**29.** This Is Fine format: Dog in burning room labeled "Me at closing when the lender calls at 11:30am to say they need one more document and we close at noon"
- Why it works: The burning room = the closing timeline. The specificity (11:30am, noon, one more document) elevates it.

**30.** Always Has Been format: "Wait, the house always had water in the crawlspace?" / "Always has been."
- Why it works: Dark twist on an inspection discovery. The astronaut format adds dramatic irony.

**31.** Drake format: Top (reject): "A Zillow listing with 47 professional photos, drone footage, 3D walkthrough, and floor plan" / Bottom (approve): "A Zillow listing with 3 photos, two of which are the same bathroom from different angles, and one is the agent's thumb"
- Why it works: The approved option is the worse one, which subverts expectations and reflects the absurd reality of MLS.

## Pattern 7: INSIDER BASEBALL (agent-to-agent only, use sparingly)

These only work for agents. Lower reach but high engagement and loyalty.

**Structure:** Reference a specific tool, process, or pain that only licensed agents experience.

### Examples:

**32.** "MLS goes down at 11pm on a Thursday when you have 3 offers due by Friday at noon. Name a more iconic duo."
- Why it works: The specific scenario that every agent has panicked through.

**33.** "The sound a lockbox makes when it doesn't open and the client is standing right there watching you pretend you definitely know what you're doing."
- Why it works: Sensory specificity (the sound), the social pressure, the pretending.

**34.** "Updating the CRM at 11pm like I'm confessing my sins. 'I did not follow up with this lead. I did not call back this listing inquiry. I have no excuse.'"
- Why it works: The confession framing is unexpected. The honest admission of failure.

**35.** "That agent who wears a full suit to the office just to use the printer."
- Why it works: Every office has this person. The pointless formality is the joke.

## Pattern 8: THE PIVOT (setup leads one direction, punchline goes another)

**Structure:** First sentence establishes an expectation. Second sentence demolishes it.

### Examples:

**36.** "Finally sold the house after 127 days on market. Sellers celebrated. Buyers celebrated. I went home and sat in the shower fully clothed."
- Why it works: Everyone else is happy. The agent is destroyed. The "fully clothed" detail is the unexpected gut punch.

**37.** "Spent 4 hours making the perfect CMA. Color-coded. Charts. Market analysis. Seller looked at it for 9 seconds and said 'But our neighbor sold theirs for more.'"
- Why it works: Effort vs. dismissal. The "9 seconds" is devastatingly specific.

**38.** "They see the closing photos. They don't see the 3am panic texts, the appraisal renegotiation, the lender who ghosted for 4 days, the seller who tried to take the washer/dryer at the last minute, and the 37 'quick questions' before bed."
- Why it works: The glossy exterior vs. the chaotic interior. Every detail is a real thing that happens.

## Using This Library

When generating a new meme concept:

1. Pick the current friction from `friction_topics.md`
2. Pick the humor pattern from this file that fits
3. Write the concept following that pattern's structural rules
4. Run it through the "Would they screenshot this?" test from `humor_calibration.md`
5. If it passes, match to a template from `templates/registry.json`
6. If it doesn't pass, pick a different pattern or add more specificity

**The most common failure mode is insufficient specificity.** When in doubt, add a number, a time, a dollar amount, or a verbatim quote.
