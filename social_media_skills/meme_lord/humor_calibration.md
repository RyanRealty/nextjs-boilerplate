# Humor Calibration — What's Funny vs. What's Corny

**This document is the single most important file in the meme lord skill.** Read it before generating any meme concept. It exists because the previous output was "corny as hell" (Matt's words). This file teaches the difference.

**Source:** Deep study of @thebrokeagent (545K, the gold standard), @therealestatememes, @re_memes, @zillowgonewild, Lighter Side of Real Estate, BAM/NowBam, and 100+ viral RE meme examples collected April 2026.

---

## The Core Problem With "Brand Memes"

Brand memes try to be funny AND look professional. That's the trap. The funniest RE meme accounts succeed because they picked funny and let professional take care of itself through competence, not through tone.

**Corny brand meme thinking:** "How do we make our brand relatable while maintaining professionalism?"

**Funny meme thinking:** "What happened to me this week that made me want to quit?"

Those two starting points produce completely different content.

## The 7 Rules of Actually Funny RE Memes

### Rule 1: Painful Specificity Over Relatable Generality

The #1 difference between viral RE memes and corny ones. Every corny meme is general. Every viral one is weirdly specific.

**CORNY:** "When you finally close the deal" + celebration gif
**FUNNY:** "When you close the deal after the buyer switched lenders twice, the appraiser came in $15K low, the seller's dog bit the inspector, and the title company found a lien from 2003"

**CORNY:** "Real estate agents work hard"
**FUNNY:** "11:47pm. In bed. Eyes closed. Phone buzzes. 'Hey quick question about that house we drove past 6 weeks ago'"

**CORNY:** "Buyers be like: 'we're just looking'"
**FUNNY:** "'We're not in any rush' — the buyer who called me 14 times on a Sunday because a house they liked got 3 offers in 2 days"

The specificity creates recognition. When an agent reads "the seller's dog bit the inspector," they don't think "that's relatable" — they think "that literally happened to me on Greenwood Ave in 2024." That's the difference between a like and a share.

**Test:** If you can swap "real estate" for "car sales" or "insurance" and the joke still works, it's too general. Kill it.

### Rule 2: The Joke Must Hurt a Little

The best memes touch a nerve. They're funny because they're true in a way that's slightly uncomfortable.

**CORNY:** "What my friends think I do vs. what I actually do" (tired format, no edge)
**FUNNY:** "My friends think I got rich selling houses. I am currently eating a gas station burrito in my car between showings and my commission check from March hasn't cleared yet."

**CORNY:** "When the offer gets accepted!" (celebration)
**FUNNY:** "Got the offer accepted. Now I just need the appraisal to come in, the buyer to not lose their job, the seller to not change their mind, the lender to not find a 2019 medical collection, and Mercury to not be in retrograde."

The pain is what makes it funny. An agent reading this feels SEEN. That's the share trigger.

### Rule 3: Punch Yourself, Not Your Clients

@thebrokeagent's entire brand is built on self-deprecation about agent life. The humor targets the agent's own suffering, not the client's stupidity.

**CORNY (punching down at clients):** "Dumb buyers who don't get pre-approved lol"
**FUNNY (punching yourself):** "Me, a professional with a license and 200 hours of continuing education, nervously googling 'what is a plat map' before a listing appointment"

**CORNY:** "Sellers who overprice their homes are the worst"
**FUNNY:** "Nodding at the seller's $900K price opinion while internally drafting my 'we need to talk about pricing' email that I will rewrite 11 times before sending"

When you punch down at clients, agents feel defensive (those are THEIR clients). When you punch yourself, agents feel solidarity.

**Exception:** Client behavior memes work when the client is clearly the HERO of their own comedy — like the Maury format: "You stated you were ready to buy. 47 showings over 4 months has determined that was a lie." The client is the star of the bit, and they'd laugh at it too.

### Rule 4: Use Real Numbers, Real Times, Real Details

Specificity is the engine. The more specific the detail, the more it reads as a real story rather than a marketing intern's idea of funny.

**CORNY:** "When a buyer sends a lowball offer"
**FUNNY:** "Listed at $725K. Offer comes in at $580K. Cover letter says 'We feel this is fair given the current market.' Brother, what market are you in."

**CORNY:** "Working on weekends"
**FUNNY:** "4th of July. 96 degrees. 15 White Claws deep. Phone rings. 'Can we see that house on Galveston at 2pm today?'"

**CORNY:** "Agents are always on their phones"
**FUNNY:** "'I don't want to bother you' — the text I received at 11:58pm on a Tuesday asking if a house with an accepted offer could still be shown"

Numbers to use: prices, times, counts of showings, days on market, percentages, temperature, number of phone calls, specific streets, specific apps (Zillow, Redfin, MLS).

### Rule 5: The Template Should Amplify, Not Carry

If the meme only works because of the template image and the text is forgettable, it's a bad meme. The text should be funny on its own. The template just makes it funnier.

**Template carrying (weak):**
Drake meme — Top: "Good listing photos" / Bottom: "Bad listing photos"
(The Drake format is doing all the work. The text is nothing.)

**Text carrying (strong):**
Drake meme — Top: "Listing with 47 professional photos, drone shots, and a Matterport" / Bottom: "Listing with 3 photos taken on an iPhone 8 through a screen door and one is just the agent's thumb"
(This is funny even without Drake. The template amplifies it.)

**Test:** Read just the text without the image. If it's not at least slightly funny as plain text, the concept is too weak.

### Rule 6: Current > Classic

The memes that go most viral reference whatever is happening RIGHT NOW in real estate.

**Always current friction points (2025-2026):**
- The NAR settlement commission conversations
- Rate lock drama ("married to the rate" became a viral phrase)
- "I'll wait for rates to drop" buyers who've been waiting since 2022
- The Zillow/Redfin/Realtor.com Zestimate disconnect
- iBuyer implosion aftermath
- The buyer-rep agreement awkwardness
- Remote workers moving to smaller markets (Bend is ground zero for this)
- TikTok house tours ("I saw this house on TikTok, can we buy it?" — it's in another state)
- AI-generated listing descriptions that all sound the same

**Stale friction points (been done to death):**
- "What my friends think I do" (dead format since 2018)
- Generic "working weekends" without specifics
- "The client who ghosts" without the specific detail that makes it fresh
- FSBO jokes (unless given a new angle)

### Rule 7: Memes Are Screenshots, Not Posters

The best memes look like they were made in 30 seconds on a phone. They're raw. Low production value IS the aesthetic. This is the opposite of brand content.

**Corny:** Perfectly designed graphic with brand colors, logo placement, custom fonts, gradient backgrounds
**Funny:** Impact font on a recognizable template, slightly rough, could've been made by anyone

When a meme looks too polished, it reads as an ad. When it looks like something a friend made, it gets shared.

**Ryan Realty application:** The `@ryanrealty` watermark stays small and subtle. No brand color borders. No logo bars. The meme must look like a meme, not a Canva template.

## The Corny-to-Funny Translation Guide

Here are real examples of how to take a corny concept and make it actually hit:

### Example 1: The Rate Wait

**CORNY:** "Don't wait for rates to drop to buy a house!"
**FUNNY:** "2022: 'I'll wait for rates to drop.' 2023: 'Any day now.' 2024: 'The Fed said maybe.' 2025: 'OK maybe I should've just bought.' 2026: 'Why does this house cost $80K more now.'"

Why it works: The escalation is specific to real years with real sentiments people actually said. It tells a story. The punchline lands at the end.

### Example 2: The Inspection

**CORNY:** "When the inspection report comes back" + shocked face
**FUNNY:** "Inspector: 'The foundation has a crack.' Me: 'How bad?' Inspector: 'Depends. Do you consider $40,000 bad?' Me: *already dialing the buyers to manage expectations while mentally calculating if my commission will even cover therapy*"

Why it works: The escalation from factual to emotional to absurd. The specific dollar amount. The self-deprecating punchline.

### Example 3: The Zestimate

**CORNY:** "Zillow says my house is worth more than it is" + woman yelling at cat
**FUNNY:** "'My Zestimate says $850K.' Ma'am, Zillow also thinks your neighbor's shed is a separate dwelling and that garage counts as a bedroom. The comps say $715K and the comps have been inside your house."

Why it works: The specific absurdity of Zillow's data quality (shed as dwelling, garage as bedroom) is something every agent has seen. The pivot to "the comps have been inside your house" is the kill shot.

### Example 4: The Open House

**CORNY:** "When nobody comes to your open house" + lonely image
**FUNNY:** "Open house from 1-3pm. Put out the sign, baked the cookies, printed the flyers. It's now 2:47pm. One person has come. It was the neighbor. She took 6 cookies and told me the previous owners were 'interesting people' and left."

Why it works: Timeline specificity (2:47pm), the detail of the cookies, the neighbor being the only visitor (every agent has this story), and the passive-aggressive "interesting people" comment.

### Example 5: The Commission

**CORNY:** "People think agents make so much money"
**FUNNY:** "My commission check: $12,400. After split: $6,200. After taxes: $4,340. After photos, staging, signs, MLS fees, lockbox, gas, Canva subscription, and the $47 worth of snacks nobody ate at the open house: I made less than the Uber driver who brought the buyer to the showing."

Why it works: The waterfall of deductions is painfully real. The specific $47 snacks detail. The Uber driver comparison is the unexpected punchline that stings.

## What NOT To Do (The Corny Checklist)

If your meme concept hits ANY of these, rethink it:

1. **The punchline is in the first line.** Setup first. Always.
2. **You could replace "real estate" with any profession.** Too generic.
3. **The humor depends entirely on the template image.** Text must be funny alone.
4. **It reads like a marketing team approved it.** If it's "safe for the company page," it's probably not funny.
5. **It uses "be like" without a specific payoff.** "Buyers be like" is not a joke. "Buyers be like 'we have no budget' and then send you a $1.2M listing at midnight" is.
6. **It explains the joke.** If the caption says "this is SO true" or "every agent knows this feeling," you're narrating instead of joking.
7. **The emoji count exceeds 2.** Zero is ideal. One is fine. More than two = trying too hard.
8. **It ends with "Am I right?" or "Who else?"** That's engagement bait dressed as humor.
9. **The observation is obvious.** "Real estate agents work weekends" is not an insight. Everyone knows this. "Real estate agents say 'I'll just check this one email' and emerge 3 hours later having restructured an entire deal" is an insight.
10. **It's a humble brag disguised as self-deprecation.** "I'm SO busy with all these closings, I can barely keep up! #blessed" is not self-deprecation. It's bragging with a wink. Actual self-deprecation has to actually hurt.

## Voice Examples From Accounts That Get It Right

### @theloanwrangler — broker/loan-side humor reference

Mortgage broker account that crosses real estate friction. Reference reel: instagram.com/reel/DUHGvdLkrDB ("Glad I didn't go to the party"). Engagement: ~960 likes, 54 comments, 30 reposts on a single reel — same working-broker scale as JKnox, with comment volume that signals the post is hitting people's actual lives (not just landing aesthetic likes).

What works in the @theloanwrangler register:
- Loan-side perspective covers the same closing chaos / lender / underwriter friction the agent feels — but from the other side of the table. Useful for memes about the lender, the underwriter, the conditional approval, the "one more document" loop.
- Same deadpan-voice register as JKnox / @thebrokeagent. Pain told flat, no exclamation points, no "AM I RIGHT" filler.
- The "Glad I didn't go to the party" framing flips the format on its head — the meme is about what the broker *avoided* by being on a closing call instead. That kind of inversion is a usable structural pattern.

Why we list it here: Matt is referencing it as a calibration target. The bar is mid-tier-engagement, working-professional voice, broker-side or agent-side pain — not aspirational lifestyle, not "luxury market commentary."

### @funnyrealestatehumor / JKnox (Dayton, OH — second benchmark)

A working agent in Dayton who posts across Facebook, TikTok, Instagram, and Pinterest. Bio: "For the funniest Real Estate Memes on the PLANET." Mid-tier follower count, but engagement is real (~1K likes / ~40 reposts on individual reels). Reference reel: instagram.com/reel/DW8nq5hjvwc

What he does that works:
- Same self-deprecating agent register as Eric Simon — agent suffering told flat.
- Cross-platform repost cadence that compounds — same image meme runs on FB, IG, TikTok, Pinterest. Volume + consistency, not virality on a single post.
- Mid-tier polish. Image memes look made on a phone, not a Canva template. Low gloss is the point.
- Hyper-current friction — rate cycle, Zillow comments, "just looking" buyers, inspection chaos, the day-of-closing gauntlet.

Why it matters for our skill: JKnox proves the pattern works at the working-agent level, not just at Eric Simon's 545K scale. The voice is what compounds. Match that voice — terse, lived-in, painful specificity — and the post does the work whether or not you have the audience yet.

### @thebrokeagent (THE benchmark, 545K followers)

Eric Simon's voice: terse, insider, self-deprecating, no emoji energy. The humor comes from lived pain told with deadpan delivery.

Patterns that recur:
- First-person agent suffering ("Me, doing [absurd thing] at [specific time]")
- Client quotes that are painfully verbatim
- The disconnect between perception and reality of agent life
- Timely market commentary delivered as gallows humor
- Memes about agents checking their phones, the CRM, and emails compulsively

What he never does:
- Explains the joke
- Uses engagement bait CTAs
- Posts overly polished branded graphics
- Punches down at clients without empathy
- Uses AI-voice words (delve, navigate, leverage, etc.)

### @zillowgonewild (2.2M followers)

Voice: deadpan. The listing itself IS the joke. Zero editorializing.

What works: They just post the absurd listing photo with minimal or no caption. The content does the work. "This $400K house comes with a toilet in the kitchen. Next to the stove."

Lesson for us: Sometimes the funniest thing is restraint. Don't over-explain. Don't add 3 hashtags and a CTA. Let the absurdity breathe.

### @therealestatememes (20K followers)

Lower follower count but high engagement per post. They lean into classic templates with RE-specific text. The best posts are hyper-specific agent-life moments.

### BAM / NowBam (Broke Agent Media)

Their top-performing posts of all time share common DNA:
- POV format (first-person perspective)
- Physical comedy about agent life
- Unexpected collaborations or cameos
- "Buyers' Dads" as a recurring character (the deal-killer in-law)

## Template-Selection Heuristic

Not all templates work for all jokes. Here's when each one actually fits vs. when it's being forced:

| Template | Works when... | Forced when... |
|---|---|---|
| Drake | Clear binary: specific bad choice vs. specific good choice | The options are vague or the "good" option is just the "professional" answer |
| Expanding Brain | True escalation — each panel more absurd than the last | The panels are just a list of things, not an escalation |
| Woman Yelling at Cat | Two-sided disagreement where both sides have a point | One side is clearly wrong and it's just "educating" |
| This Is Fine | A situation that is objectively falling apart but the person is pretending it's OK | Any generic stressful situation |
| Distracted Boyfriend | Choosing the wrong option while the right one watches | The three labels don't have genuine tension |
| Change My Mind | A genuinely provocative take that will split the audience | A take everyone already agrees with |
| Gru's Plan | A plan that seems smart until the third step reveals the flaw | The plan doesn't have a genuine "oh no" moment |
| Two Buttons | A genuine impossible choice with no good answer | One button is clearly right |

## The "Would They Screenshot This?" Test

Before any meme ships, ask this one question:

**"Would an agent screenshot this and send it to their group chat with no caption?"**

If the answer is yes, ship it. The screenshot-to-group-chat behavior is the strongest signal of genuine humor. It means the meme doesn't need explanation, it doesn't need the sender to add context, and it's funny enough that someone wants credit for sharing it.

If the meme needs a "lol so true" from the sender to work, it's not funny enough.
